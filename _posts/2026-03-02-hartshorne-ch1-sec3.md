**Exercise 3.18 (Projective Normal Varieties).**
A projective variety $Y\subseteq \mathbb{P}^n$ is projective normal if $S(Y)$ is integrally closed.
(a) If $Y$ is projective normal, then $Y$ is normal.
(b) There are normal varieties in projective space which are not projective normal. For example, let $Y$ be the twisted quartic curve in $\mathbb{P}^3$ given by $(t^4,t^3u,tu^3,u^4)$.
(c) Show that the twisted quartic curve $Y$ above is isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^1$, which is projective normal.

*Proof.*
(a) If $S(Y)$ is an integrally closed domain, then for any point $P$, the local ring $\mathcal{O}_P$, which is the degree 0 part of the localization $S(Y)_P$, is also integrally closed. Hence $Y$ is normal.

(b) $S(Y)=k[t^4,t^3u,tu^3,u^4]$. Consider the element $t^2u^2$ in the fraction field. It satisfies the monic equation $X^2 - (t^4)(u^4) = 0$, so it is integral over $S(Y)$, but $t^2u^2 \notin S(Y)$. Thus, $S(Y)$ is not integrally closed.
However, taking the affine cover:
On $U_1$ ($t=1$), the coordinate ring is generated by $(u,u^3,u^4)$, which is just $k[u]$ (a UFD, hence integrally closed).
On $U_2$ ($u=1$), the coordinate ring is $k[t]$.
Since it is locally integrally closed, $Y$ is a normal variety.

(c) Define $\psi :\mathbb{P}^1\rightarrow\mathbb{P}^3$ by $[t:s]\mapsto[t^4:t^3s:ts^3:s^4]$. This is a well-defined morphism and bijective. Locally, it induces isomorphisms (e.g., $k[u] \to k[u]$). Since it is a local isomorphism, it is an isomorphism of varieties. $\square$

**Exercise 3.19.**
Let $Y$ be a variety of dimension $\ge 2$, and let $P\in Y$ be a normal point. Let $f$ be a regular function on $Y-P$.
(a) Show that $f$ extends to a regular function on $Y$.
(b) Show this would be false for $\dim Y=1$.

*Proof.*
(a) Since $f$ is regular on $Y-P$, we can view $f \in K(Y)$.
Now, $P$ is a normal point, hence the local ring $\mathcal{O}_P$ is an integrally closed domain. By algebraic properties of integrally closed domains, 
$$\mathcal{O}_P = \bigcap_{\mathrm{ht}(\mathfrak{q})=1} (\mathcal{O}_P)_{\mathfrak{q}}$$
Let $Q$ be the generic point of the closed subvariety corresponding to $\mathfrak{q}$. Since $\dim Y \ge 2$, the height 1 prime $\mathfrak{q}$ cannot be the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_P$.
Thus $Q \neq P$, which means $Q \in Y-P$.
By assumption, $f$ is regular on $Y-P$, so $f \in \mathcal{O}_Q = (\mathcal{O}_P)_{\mathfrak{q}}$ for all such $Q$.
Therefore, $f \in \mathcal{O}_P$, meaning $f$ extends to a regular function at $P$.

(b) Let $Y=\mathbb{A}^1$ and $P=0$. The function $f(z)=\frac{1}{z}$ is regular on $\mathbb{A}^1-\{0\}$, but it cannot be extended to $P=0$ because it has a pole there. $\square$

**Exercise 3.20 (Automorphisms of $\mathbb{A}^n$).**
Let $\rho:\mathbb{A}^n\rightarrow\mathbb{A}^n$ be a morphism given by $n$ polynomials $f_1,...,f_n$ of $n$ variables $x_1,...,x_n$. Let $J=\det |\partial f_i/\partial x_j|$ be the Jacobian polynomial of $\rho$.
(a) If $\rho$ is an isomorphism show that $J$ is a nonzero constant polynomial.

*Proof.*
(a) Since $\rho$ is an isomorphism, there exists an inverse morphism $\psi$ given by polynomials $g_1, \dots, g_n$. By the chain rule, the product of their Jacobian matrices is the identity matrix $I$. 
Taking the determinant, we get $J(\rho) \cdot J(\psi) = 1$.
Since $J(\rho)$ and $J(\psi)$ are polynomials in $k[x_1, \dots, x_n]$, they must be units in the polynomial ring. The only units in a polynomial ring over a field are the nonzero constants. Hence $J$ is a nonzero constant polynomial. $\square$

**Exercise 3.21 (Group variety).**

*Proof.*
(a) For $G_a$, the map $\mu $ is given by the ring homomorphism $k[t]\rightarrow k[x,y]$ mapping $t\mapsto x+y$, hence it is a morphism. It acts as a group because $k$ itself is an abelian group under addition, and the inverse map $t\mapsto -t$ is a morphism.
(b) For $G_m$, the map $\mu $ is given by $k[t,t^{-1}]\rightarrow k[x,x^{-1},y,y^{-1}]$ mapping $t\mapsto xy$. Because $k^*$ is an abelian group under multiplication, it is a group, and the inverse map $t\mapsto t^{-1}$ is a morphism.
(c) $\mathrm{Hom}(X,G)$ has a natural group structure defined point-wise by $x\mapsto \mu(f(x),g(x))$. It is a group because $\mu$ gives the group structure (associativity, identity). The inverse is given by mapping $x\mapsto I(f(x))$, where $I$ is the inverse morphism of $G$.
(d) $\mathrm{Hom}(X,\mathbb{A}^1)$ is exactly $\mathcal{O}(X)$ as a set. Since $\mathbb{A}^1$ has a natural additive structure, we have $\mathrm{Hom}(X,G_a)\simeq \mathcal{O}(X)$ as an abelian group.
(e) $\mathrm{Hom}(X,G_m)\simeq\mathcal{O}(X)^*$ by definition. It is a subset of $\mathrm{Hom}(X,\mathbb{A}^1)$ where nowhere-vanishing is required, and all elements of $\mathrm{Hom}(X,G_m)$ correspond to units in the coordinate ring. Hence $\mathrm{Hom}(X,G_m)\simeq\mathcal{O}(X)^*$. $\square$
